SAT Scores Data
State Demographics Data

The SAT Scores data set provides a comprehensive overview of a decade’s worth of SAT score averages from 2005- 2015 broken down by state. The data set provides a frequency distribution on income brackets and breaks down average scores by these family income brackets, namely: less than 20k, 20-40k, 40-60k, 60-80k, and more than 100k. Additionally, the data set provides average scores as well as a frequency distribution of score ranges grouped by gender. Furthermore, there is some additional context of academic strength in the form of average GPA and average years taken of Arts/Music, English, Foreign Languages, Mathematics, Natural Sciences, and Social Sciences, but these will not be utilized as heavily in the tutorial. Instead, we will look to identify correlations by combining the SAT scores from 2007 - 2014 with the CORGIS State Demographics data set.

The States Demographics data set gives a view of information collected from the United States Census Bureau over the years 2007 - 2014, however the information provided is a condensed and summarized set of data from 2015 broken up between all 50 of the states. The data set differentiates between those whose education ended with a high school degree or above compared to those who have attained a bachelor's degree or above. Employment is classified as farm or non-farm related, and it includes a percent change for the non-farm sector to give context to what areas are urbanizing. Beyond exploring the well-established trend of income vs. average score, we can investigate how much a change from one income bracket to another affects SAT score improvements i.e. are there diminishing returns, or if it affects both verbal and math scores equally. The data set also contains information pertaining to languages spoken in a household, firm ownership/employment, and home ownership, among other things. These miscellaneous points of data can be used to build a profile of the type of environment that creates higher standardized test scores, or how higher test scores can be indicative of higher/lower values in these areas.

Questions to Consider for the Combined SAT Scores and State Demographics Data:
</p>

  • What is the relationship between prevalence of farms and average SAT scores? Does it affect male and female scores in the same way?
  • Do states with a higher presence of bachelor degrees or higher perform better on the SATs?
  • Do states with a higher home ownership rate perform better on the SATs?
  • Do states with a lower person per household ratio perform better on the SATs?
  • Do states with a higher rate of living stability (i.e. rate of households that have been living in the same house 1+ years) perform better on the SATs?
  • Do states with a lower average commute time perform better on the SATs?
  • Do states with a higher rate of languages other than English at home typically see a lower SAT verbal score?
  • Do states with higher average female scores typically see a higher rate of female-owned firms?

------------------------------NORMALIZATION EXPLAINED------------------------------¶

Question: Why do you use this sort of normalization? Why not normalize in the standard way with the average reading score across all states?

If we used the standard normalization, states with lower quality education systems that also see a higher prevalence of non-English languages at home would still appear to underperform simply due to the lower quality education system because they are already performing below average, so the standard normalization does not do a good job of eliminating this collinearity. When we use the transformed % of average math score, we know that states with lower qualtiy education systems will be performing worse on the math section anyway, so it eliminates this collinearity worry to a good extent. Recall are looking to see if a higher % of other language at home explains the underperformance of average reading score relative to average math score. See the bold section below for more detail.

Rather than identifying current relevant data and/or loading in new data and performing a multilinear regression to obtain values for predicted average verbal score, which will most certainly not be able to consider every single aspect that may affect real average verbal score, we are simply using the following idea:¶

PREDICTED AVERAGE VERBAL SCORE = REAL AVERAGE MATH SCORE¶

Why can we do this?¶

The correlation between the two is .98. It is basically the best way to model predicted average verbal score. Nearly every aspect relating to wealth, strength of education system, etc. on a state-by-state basis will be encapsulated in the real average math score. Some points, however, deviate from this prediction, but what factors might explain this?: such factors that do not affect average math score but could affect averageverbal score, e.g. lower exposure to the English language at home.¶

Let's explore one more question from our list:

  • Do states with a more stable home life perform better on the SAT?

This is a bit of an open-ended question. We know what it means to perform better on the SAT, that's simply a higher average total score. But what is a more stable home life vs. a less stable home life? We are going to use two arbitrary but reasonable criteria in our definition:

  1. A higher rate of homeowners who have owned and lived in the same house for 1+ years is indicative of a state with a generally more stable home life than one with a lower rate. The same house 1+ years criterion clearly relates to our notion of "stability" in home life: same area, same school, same people, same activities, etc. in the student's life for a longer and continuous period of time. Homeownership implies that the family has intentions of remaining there and can be associated with stability.
  2. A lower average commute time is indicative of a state with a generally more stable home life than a state with a higher average commute time. We are making the assumption that if a parent has a lower commute time and thus more free time, the parent will spend some of this time with his/her child and that this is beneficial to the child's academic success.

So we are assuming here that staying in the same home, and thus usually the same school district/community, is beneificial to a student's academic success. We are also assuming that parents who have lower commute times spend more time with their children on average, and this is beneficial to a child's academic success (should be true especially in the earlier years of schooling.) These are both reasonable assumptions.

Let's pull the relevant columns. The State Demographics data set contains average commute times, homeownership rates, and living in same house 1+ years, which is everything that we are looking for to combine with the SAT scores. We will also create a calculated column called Stable Home Rate that is Same House 1+ Years * Homeownership Rate as a percentage. Essentially, it is the percentage of households that own their home and have been living there for 1 or more years.

INSIGHT AND POLICY DECISION¶

We saw that median household income and average total score do not have a strong positive correlation on the state level, so we were looking to see what other factors might help explain such drastic differences between scores from state to state. % Other Language at Home was one variable we investigated, and our analysis weakly - moderately suggests that having a higher % language other than English at home in a state does negatively impact the SAT verbal score in general. This analysis was done moreso to get some insight rather than suggest authoritarian policy decisions on what languages you can and can not speak at home.

Additionally, we looked at average commute time and average stable home rate. The heatmap suggests that, indeed, a lower average commute time and higher stable home rate is generally beneficial to a student's performance on the SATs. Here, policy insight might be a little more applicable. States may be able to implement policies that encourage homeownership and higher stability of living situations, and they may be able to lower average commute times through improvements to highways, roads, etc. The specifics of such policy decisions are best left to the experts who can more appropriately evaluate possibilities and cost/benefits of related legislation.
